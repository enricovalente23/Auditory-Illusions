Binaural Beats

This acoustic illusion is perceived when two different pure-tone
sine waves are presented to a listener dichotically (one through
each ear), with f2 slightly off 2f1.

As a result, a single tone with frequency |2f1 - f2| and
modulated amplitude is perceived. These beats are called
"binaural" because they are originated in the brain:
in the ears, the regions of the cochlea that are responsive
to the two frequencies are far apart.
Hence, note that in this demonstration earphones are
required in order to correctly perceive the effect.

In the first place, two different tones are individually
presented to each ear. Then the two tones are played
simultaneously, reaching after few seconds the frequency
ratio required to ear the beats (oscillating amplitude).

The Shepard Tone

The Shepard Tone is an auditory illusion of a tone that seems to continually
ascend or descend in pitch, yet which ultimately gets no higher or lower.
Here, the effect is presented in a glissando fashion: a continuous
never-ending rising sound is perceived.

Play with us to learn how in works!
Press W to jump and S to duck.
In the lower box an oscilloscope reveals how the infinite scale is made: the illusion
comes from the superposition of 10 dephased oscillators with rising frequencies.
Each bell shape is an oscillator, they are made by visible rectangles only because
of the choice of the FFT size.

Hitting a red obstacle focuses the oscilloscope on a single oscillator, highlighting its behaviour.

The ultimate effect is given by fading in and out each scale/oscillator once it reaches a certain
frequency lower or upper bound, so that hearing the beginning or end of any given scale
is impossible and a continuous sound is perceived.
